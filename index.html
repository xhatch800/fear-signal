Top 5 contested fears today

#1 most pervasive today

US-Iran military escalation conflict risk

Reach

Ongoing ceasefire near expiration with U.S. naval blockade ongoing and peace talks stalled.

The fear

Resumption of full-scale military conflict between United States and Iran after two-week ceasefire expires, with potential regional spillover effects.

Why it's contested

Some analysts view threats as credible deterrents while others see them as negotiating tactics unlikely to result in renewed major fighting. Expert disagreement exists on whether Trump administration prefers war or deal-making despite hostile rhetoric.

The strongest cases

Why some find it legitimate

Both sides have exchanged explicit threats; Iran has fired at commercial shipping; strategic infrastructure remains at risk; ceasefire terms fundamentally disputed by both parties.

Why others find it exaggerated

Trump has extended ceasefire and shown preference for deal-making; both sides retain incentive to negotiate; military costs and economic pressures limit appetite for sustained conflict.

Questions worth sitting with

  • Will the April 25 ceasefire expiration trigger major escalation or renewed negotiations?
  • How credible are Trump's threats to attack Iranian civilian infrastructure?
  • Can diplomacy succeed when both sides have incompatible core demands?

#2 most pervasive today

AI investment bubble and financial crash risk

Reach

Tech sector AI spending exceeds $400 billion in 2024 with massive debt-funded infrastructure expansion.

The fear

Artificial intelligence sector overinvestment may trigger financial market crash comparable to 2000 dot-com bubble when revenue fails to materialize.

Why it's contested

Proponents argue AI returns will eventually justify investments and tech giants have sufficient cash to sustain spending; skeptics contend massive debt financing of unprofitable companies creates unsustainable risk.

The strongest cases

Why some find it legitimate

Gap between massive capital expenditure and minimal revenues widening; OpenAI unprofitable until 2030; many AI pilots showing no return on investment; electricity supply cannot meet data center demand in many locations.

Why others find it exaggerated

AI revenue already substantial at $60 billion annually; tech giants have deep cash reserves and ability to weather downturns; technology ultimately transformative justifying current investments.

Questions worth sitting with

  • Will AI revenue generation accelerate enough to justify current investment levels?
  • Can unprofitable AI companies sustain debt financing for 4+ more years?
  • Is the electricity constraint temporary or structural barrier to scaling?

#3 most pervasive today

Water scarcity weaponization in South Asia

Reach

Nuclear-armed India and Pakistan turning shared river systems into geopolitical leverage amid governance vacuum.

The fear

Water scarcity conflicts between India and Pakistan could trigger military escalation or humanitarian catastrophe given suspension of water treaties and dam disputes.

Why it's contested

Some experts view water stress as long-term structural issue unlikely to provoke near-term conflict; others see loaded weapons and removed guardrails as creating imminent flashpoint risk.

The strongest cases

Why some find it legitimate

Indus Waters Treaty suspended; no binding agreements on major dams; both nations nuclear-armed; history of militarization over water rights; extremists exploit water scarcity for recruitment.

Why others find it exaggerated

Interstate water disputes take years to escalate militarily; economic interdependence creates negotiation incentives; international community maintains pressure for dialogue.

Questions worth sitting with

  • Will failed water treaty renegotiations lead to military action or continued diplomatic stalling?
  • How close is Pakistan to internal water crisis that forces aggressive external actions?
  • Can international mediation prevent escalation given great power disengagement?

#4 most pervasive today

Stagflation from Middle East oil shocks

Reach

Strait of Hormuz closure risks pushed Brent crude above $100 per barrel in March 2026.

The fear

Persistent energy price inflation combined with economic slowdown could trigger stagflation, the difficult combination of rising prices and slowing growth.

Why it's contested

Some see current inflation as temporary pending conflict resolution; others view structural factors and supply disruptions as indicating persistent price pressures.

The strongest cases

Why some find it legitimate

Brent crude spiked above $100 for first time since 2022; UK inflation estimates rising to 3-3.5% despite ceasefire; central banks shifted from rate-cut expectations to hold stance; energy supply recovery slow even if conflict ends.

Why others find it exaggerated

Ceasefire extended by Trump suggesting preference for deal; global growth projections still above 2% IMF forecast; inflationary pressures were subdued before conflict escalation.

Questions worth sitting with

  • Will the Strait of Hormuz remain restricted after ceasefire expiration?
  • How much does energy inflation feed through to broader price level versus remaining commodities-specific?
  • Can central banks manage stagflation without triggering deeper recession?

#5 most pervasive today

Democratic integrity threats in 2026 midterms

Reach

Election experts report heightened concerns about federal government interference in voting processes nationwide.

The fear

Federal law enforcement or political interference could disrupt 2026 midterm election legitimacy through voter intimidation, ballot seizures, or result contestation.

Why it's contested

Experts express cautious optimism about preventing most extreme scenarios while still concerned about unprecedented federal activism in election administration. Disagreement exists on likelihood and severity of potential interventions.

The strongest cases

Why some find it legitimate

Trump administration pushing federal election law changes; federal agents showing up at polling places causing expert concern; executive orders under litigation; court rulings on validity still pending.

Why others find it exaggerated

Federal courts have blocked most aggressive deployments; many states have part-time legislatures limiting new laws; Supreme Court previously protective of election administration; overturning elections faces structural obstacles.

Questions worth sitting with

  • Will federal agents actually appear at polling places in significant numbers?
  • Can courts block sufficient election law changes to preserve 2026 legitimacy?
  • What will happen if Trump contests midterm results on federal grounds?
